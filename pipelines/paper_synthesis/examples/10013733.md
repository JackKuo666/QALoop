## INTRODUCTION

In many areas of the Caribbean, local supply of fish obtained from capture fisheries cannot meet market demand, which generally exceeds total potential catch for high-value, nearshore species (Goodwin et al. 1985; Tucker and Jory 199 1). Aquaculture is a means to increase seafood supply, yet has been relatively slow to develop in the Caribbean. The only economically successful commercial finfish aquaculture ventures, excluding aquarium fish culture, have involved the culture of freshwater species (Ryther et al. 1991). Attempts at commercial food-fish mariculture has focused on exotic, euryhaline species: tilapia and red drum, Sciaenops ocellatus (Tucker and Jory 1991). Few data on the culture potential of indigenous Caribbean marine finfish are found in the literature.

A number of members of the genus Trachinotus (Carangidae) are considered to be among the finest food fish in many parts of the world (Bardach et al. 1972; Jory et al. 1985). The pompano, 7: carolinus, received considerable attention as a mariculture candidate in the 1960's and 1970's due to high market price and inconsistent supply of wild fish. Although the nutritional requirements of pompano have not bcen determined, commercially-available salmonid diets have proven superior to other diets (Finucane 1970, 1971; Bardach et al. 1972; Tatum 1972).

The palometa, Z goodei, commonly inhabits the surf zone on exposed shores from Massachusetts to Argentina and attains a length of at least 37 cm (Randall 1983). Juveniles and adults are opportunistic feeders but consume primarily invertebrates and small fishes (Randall 1967). Palometa share many characteristics that make pompano a good rnariculture candidate and appear to be a suitable species for culture where pompano do not occur (Jory et al. 1985). Commercially-available diets need to be evaluated for the culture of palometa prior to in-depth nutritional studies. This study evaluated three salrnonid diets and one diet formulated for



juvenile warmwater marine finfish. Growth rate, feed conversion ratio, condition factor, protein efficiency ratio, and survival of palometa fed each diet were compared.

## MATERIALS AND METHODS

A flow-through system comprised of twelve $2-\mathrm{m}^{3}$ fiberglass tanks, each equipped with a 5.1-cm diameter PVC central standpipe, was utilized in this study. A 7.6-cm PVC pipe with screened orifices at the base was fined over each standpipe to facilitate solids removal. Each tank was covered with $80\%$ shade cloth. Water was continually pumped from the ocean using a I-hp pump at a rate of 20 L/min/tank. Dissolved oxygen was maintained between 5.5 and $6.8\mathrm{mg}/\mathrm{L}$$\text{(mean}=6.0\mathrm{mg}/\mathrm{L})$ and water temperature ranged from 25.8 to $27.9^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$$\left(\text{mean}=26.6^{\circ}\mathrm{C}\right)$ Mean values for salinity and pH were 35 ppt and 8.3, respectively.

Four treatments, consisting of four formulated diets (pellet size $=4$ mm), were replicated three times and randomly assigned to tanks. Three diets were formulated for salmonids. Diet 1 (FLT) was a floating diet and contained $41\%$ protein and $10\%$ moisture; Diet 2 (SMT) was a semi-moist sinking pellet and contained $44\%$ protein and $21\%$ moisture; Diet 3 (SNK) was a sinking pellet and contained $46\%$ protein and $10\%$ moisture. The fourth diet (MFF), an experimental sinking pellet formulated for juvenile warmwater marine finfish, contained $60\%$ protein and $10\%$ moisture. Proximate composition of diets was determined by an independent laboratory (Woodson-Tenent Laboratories, Inc., Memphis, Tennessee') for percentages of protein, fiber, ash, and fat on a dry-weight basis (Table 1).

Juvenile palometa were captured with a $1.8\mathrm{~m}\times30.5\mathrm{~m}$ seine with a 3.18 cm mesh (stretch) on $\mathbf{S t}$ Croix, U.S. Virgin Islands over a 27-day period. The fish were held in tanks at 5 to 11 $\mathrm{m}^{3}$ until a sufficient number of fish was obtained to conduct the experiment. During the holding period, the fish were fed a mixture of $50\%$ anchovies (Engraulididae), $40\%$ shrimp, and $10\%$ squid. The ingredients were ground, mixed, and supplemented with vitamins and minerals. When 120 fish of similar weight were obtained, a pelleted training diet that consisted of equal portions of the four experimental diets was offered to the fish. Acclimation was achieved over a four-day period by increasing the proportion of the training diet in the daily feed ration until $100\%$ pellets was offered. During the holding period, fish were fed to satiation once or twice daily.

Ten palometa $\text{(mean}\pm\mathrm{SE}$ weight, $\text{mean}\pm\mathrm{SE}$ length, and $\text{mean}\pm\mathrm{SE}$ K of $85\pm5\mathrm{~g}$ $85\pm5\mathrm{~g},153\pm8\mathrm{~mm}$ and $2.37\pm0.02$ respectively) were placed into each tank and fed for 112 days.

Fish were fed four times during daylight hours using spring-operated belt feeders (Zeigler Brothers, Gardners, Pennsylvania). Daily feed ration, based on the fish biomass in each tank and the dry weight of each diet, ranged from $5\%$ of biomass initially to $2\%$ of biomass by the end of the experiment. Growth was monitored by weighing all fish from each tank at 28-day intervals. On sample days, tanks werc cleaned; fish received a three-minute fresh water dip to control parasites; feed was withheld; and daily feed rations were adjusted.

At the end of the experiment, fork length and weight of each fish was measured, and final condition factor was calculated. Absolute growth rates (AGR; $\mathrm{R};\mathrm{g}/\mathrm{day},\mathrm{~mm}/\mathrm{day})$ were determined by dividing weight $(\mathrm{g})$ or length gain (mm) by time (days). Relative growth rate $R,\%$ $\%$ was calculated by dividing weight gain by initial weight $\left(W_{i}\right)$ multiplied by 100. Feed conversion ratio (FCR) was approximatcd by dividing total dry weight of feed offered during the experiment by total wet weight gain of fish. Protein efficiency ratio (PER) was approximated by dividing the wet weight gain of fish by the dry weight of protein fed over the experimental period.

Thirty-six fish (nine from each treatment) with a mean $\pm\mathrm{SE}$ weight of $396\pm7\mathrm{~g}$ were eviscerated and had gills removed and then weighed again to obtain percent dress-out. An additional 36 fish (nine from each treat-



ment) $\text{(mean}\pm\mathrm{SE}$ weight of $385\pm11\mathrm{~g}$ were filleted, and the percent fillet dress-out, with and without skin, was calculated.

Treatment means for initial weight, initial length, final weight, final length, AGR, RGR, FCR, PER, initial and final $\mathbf{K},$ and survival were compared by one-way analysis of variance. Pairwise multiple comparisons were made using Student-Newman-Keuls method. Treatment means were considered significant at the 0.05 level of probability. All statistical analyses were conducted with Sigma Stat (1992) statistical software.

## RESULTS

Initial weight, length, and condition of palometa did not significantly differ among treatment means. Weight gain was similar among dietary treatments (Figure I). Diet had no significant effect on final weight, final length, AGR, RGR, or FCR (Table 2), and respective combined treatment means were 399.8 g, 237.9 rnm, 2.81 g/day, 0.76 mmlday, $371.0\%$ and 2.15, respectively. At the conclusion of the study, palometa fed SMT $(\mathrm{K}=$ 3.04) and MFF $(\mathrm{K}=3.05)$ were significantly $(P<0.05)$ more robust than fish fed FLT $(\mathrm{K}=2.83)$ The condition of fish fed SNK $(\mathrm{K}=2.93)$ did not significantly differ $(P>0.05)$ from that of fish fed the other three diets. PER of fish fed FLT (1.12), SMT (1.06) or SNK (1.02) were not significantly different. The mean PER of fish fed MFF (0.77) was significantly lower than that of fish fed the other diets. Survival ranged from $93\%$ to $100\%$ with no significant differences among treatment means.

Meanf $\pm\mathrm{SE}$ dress-out for gilled and eviscerated fish was $88.4\pm0.2\%$ while dress-out for filleted fish was $55.6\pm0.5\%$ for fillets with skin and $50.2\pm0.5\%$ for fillets without skin. Statistical analyses was not conducted on percent dress-out of fish, due to a handling error which resulted in an inability to identify fish from each treatment.

Fish fed the floating (FLT) diet, which contained the lowest protein level $.4$ displayed the lowest mean final $\mathbf{K}$ but final weight, growth rate, and FCR values were similar to fish fed the other diets. The use of floating diets may reduce feed loss when used in cage culture (Thouard et al. 1990; Wu and Lee 1990).

Gornez and C e ~ i g o n (1984) found that palometa have higher growth rates during the first five months of culture than either the pompano or permit and suggested that it may be the best candidate of the three species for commercial culture in Venezuela. Market size of palometa in some areas of the Caribbean region is between 200 to $300\mathrm{~g}$ (Gomez and Cervigon 1984; Jory et al. 1985). With good environmental conditions and diets, it may be possible to raise two crops per year for local supply. Local market value of palometa in this weight range needs to be determined. Because palometa is similar in appearance to pompano, the former could be cultured to a larger size and marketed in the United States, especially for the fillet market.

Mortalities in the present study occurred due to parasitosis by the monogenean trematode Neobenedenia melleni. Symptoms fmt appeared in week 3 of the experiment and included "flashing" or rubbing against the boaom or sides of tanks, lethargy, decreased feeding activity, and corneal opacity. Parasites were controlled by a one-time, one-hour bath of 167 ppm formalin and routine three-minute freshwater dips when fish were sampled. Other researchers have reported parasitic infection of palometa by N. melleni (Gallet de Saint Aurin et al. 1990; Thouard et al. 1990) as well as other wannwater marine finfish (MacCallum 1927; Mueller et al. 1994). Methods for controlling this parasite under experimental conditions have included dip treatments in hyposaline water, copper sulfate and formalin, and trichlorfon incorporated in feed (Mueller et al. 1994). Practical methods for controlling this parasite in various culture systems need to be developed.

This study demonstrates that palometa have high growth rates and efficient feed conversion and protein utilization when fed commercially prepared salmonid diets, both sinking and floating, with protein levels as low as $41\%$ Palomcta also have excellent dressout characteristics for both gilled and eviscerated fish, as well as for fillets.

Research on defining the nutritional requirements of palometa and practical diet formulations for this species should be conducted. Knowledge gained from research on pompano may be used to further explore the culture potential of the palometa.
